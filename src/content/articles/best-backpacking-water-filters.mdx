Picking the best backpacking water filter used to be a real decision with real losers. It isn't
anymore: the three hollow-fiber squeeze filters that dominate the category — the Sawyer Squeeze,
Katadyn BeFree, and Platypus QuickDraw — all weigh within an ounce of each other, all sit in the
same budget price band, and all reliably stop the bacteria and protozoa that matter in North
American backcountry water.

What actually separates them is everything the spec sheet doesn't lead with: how they're cleaned
in the field, what happens to flow rate as they clog, which bottles they lock you into, and how
they fail. That's what this comparison is about.

## The three filters, honestly compared

<ProductSection product={frontmatter.products[0]} index={0}>
  The QuickDraw is Platypus's answer to a decade of watching people modify Sawyers, and the two
  fixes it ships are the two that matter. First, maintenance: restoring flow is a shake-and-swish
  routine done at the water source, no syringe, no clean-water sacrifice. Second, the bag
  interface locks positively, addressing the classic squeeze-filter failure of a pouch letting
  go mid-squeeze — owner reviews rarely report blow-offs.

  The honest asterisk is the 0.2 micron pore rating, wider than the 0.1 of the other two. Both
  ratings are effective against bacteria and protozoa, so this is a specification difference more
  than a safety difference, but we list it because we list what's published. The proprietary
  dirty-side threading is the more practical annoyance: you'll use Platypus's bag or an adapter
  rather than any bottle in the hiker box.
</ProductSection>

<ProductSection product={frontmatter.products[1]} index={1}>
  The Squeeze is the incumbent, and the reasons are structural. Its 28 mm threading fits the
  disposable bottles most thru-hikers already carry, so the filter is the only proprietary part
  in your water system. Nothing else in the category has its depth of trail knowledge: every
  fix, coupling trick, and failure mode is documented somewhere, which is worth more than a spec
  advantage when something goes wrong forty miles from a road.

  Its weaknesses are equally well documented. Flow rate degrades unless you backflush properly,
  and a proper backflush wants the syringe nobody carries. And the included squeeze pouches are
  the most consistently criticized item in owner reviews — the standard advice is to treat them
  as packaging and squeeze from a standard disposable bottle instead. Sawyer publishes no liter
  rating for cartridge life, so we don't print one.
</ProductSection>

<ProductSection product={frontmatter.products[2]} index={2}>
  The BeFree wins the two numbers that define fast-and-light use: a listed 2.2 oz with its flask,
  and the fastest fresh-cartridge flow in both owner reports and published third-party testing.
  The intended workflow is different from the other two — scoop, drink at the source, move —
  and for fastpacking or low-carry styles it's the least friction of the three by a margin.

  The costs are specific. The 42 mm wide-mouth threading commits you to HydraPak-style soft
  flasks, which are consumables: owner reviews report seam and wear failures, and replacements
  are not cheap. More significantly, flow-rate loss in silty or algae-heavy water is the single
  most consistent complaint in BeFree owner reviews, and swish-cleaning only partially recovers
  it. Clear mountain water: excellent. Desert cattle tanks: bring something else.
</ProductSection>

## How to choose between them

If you want the shortest possible answer: buy the QuickDraw if you value easy maintenance, the
Squeeze if you value ecosystem and the lowest price, the BeFree if you value speed and weight
and your water sources run clear.

The longer answer is to pick by your dominant failure mode. Filters don't die dramatically;
they slowly clog into uselessness because cleaning them was annoying, or they crack in a
November cold snap because they were in a side pocket instead of a sleeping bag. The QuickDraw
directly attacks the first failure mode. No filter survives the second — all three
manufacturers say the same thing: once frozen wet, replace the cartridge.

Weight is the least useful deciding factor here, which is unusual advice from us. The spread
between all three systems is under an ounce — noise compared to the water itself, at roughly
35 oz per liter. Cutting your average carry by half a liter through smarter source planning
saves more weight than any filter choice. That kind of thinking compounds across your kit: it's
the same logic as the [stove and fuel math](/gear/cooking/best-canister-stoves-backpacking/) and
the reason a lighter [shelter](/gear/shelters/best-ultralight-trekking-pole-tents/) changes how
much pack suspension you need — covered in our
[backpack fitting guide](/guides/how-to-fit-a-backpack/).

## The routine that keeps a filter alive

Whichever you buy, the difference between a filter that lasts a season and one that lasts
years is a small set of habits, all drawn from the manufacturers' own care instructions:

**Keep a dirty side and a clean side, permanently.** One bottle or bag only ever touches
source water; your drinking vessels only ever touch filtered water. Cross-threading the dirty
bag onto your clean bottle once undoes the filter's entire job. Mark the dirty container with
a strip of tape so the rule survives a tired evening.

**Clean before flow forces you to.** All three makers describe restoring flow the same way —
push or swish clean water backward through the fibers — and all three note it works best done
routinely rather than heroically. The practical cadence from owner reports: a quick backflush
or shake-clean every few days in clear water, every day in questionable water. A filter that's
"suddenly slow" has usually been quietly clogging for a week.

**Winter changes the rules.** Below freezing, the filter sleeps in your insulation and rides
in an inside pocket during the day. This is not optional care advice; it's the one failure
mode with no recovery and no visible symptom. If a filter may have frozen while wet, all three
manufacturers say the same thing: replace the cartridge and treat the old one as compromised.

**Carry the nearly-free backup.** A few chemical tablets weigh almost nothing and turn a
cracked filter from a trip-ender into an inconvenience. This is standard advice across every
long-trail organization for a reason — redundancy for water is cheap; redundancy for almost
nothing else in your pack is.

**Know when the cartridge is done.** Rated life (where published) assumes clear water; silty
sources shorten it dramatically and no cleaning fully restores hollow fibers scarred by grit.
When backflushing stops recovering flow, that's the filter telling you its number came up
early. Replacement cartridges cost far less than the giardia gamble of "probably fine."

Every weight and rating above is the manufacturer's published figure, recorded with sources in
our research file. Where a manufacturer publishes nothing — Sawyer's cartridge life — the table
says so rather than repeating a number the internet made up.

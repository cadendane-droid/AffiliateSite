A trekking-pole tent is the single biggest weight cut most backpackers ever make: the poles are
already in your hands, so the shelter stops paying their weight penalty twice. The three shelters
here — the Durston X-Mid 2, the Zpacks Duplex, and the 3F UL Gear Lanshan 2 — are the three most
common answers to "which trekking pole tent should I buy," and they represent three genuinely
different bets: one on livability, one on absolute weight, one on price.

All three are two-door, two-vestibule, two-person shelters pitched with two trekking poles. The
differences that matter are wall construction, fabric behavior in sustained rain, and what happens
to the price band. Here's how they separate.

## Where each shelter wins

<ProductSection product={frontmatter.products[0]} index={0}>
  The X-Mid's design argument is simple: the fly pitches first, off a plain rectangle of four
  stakes, and everything else hangs from it. Set up in rain, the inner tent never gets wet. Broken
  down in rain, same story. The offset-pole geometry also removes the pole-in-the-doorway problem
  older trekking-pole designs had.

  The fabric choice is quietly the bigger deal. Polyester doesn't absorb water the way nylon does,
  so the pitch you set at dusk is the pitch you wake up to — owner reviews repeatedly single out
  not having to re-tension in overnight rain. The cost is weight: at a manufacturer-listed 39.5 oz
  trail weight, it's more than double the Duplex. For most people moving from a freestanding tent,
  that's still a cut of a pound or more, with none of the single-wall learning curve.
</ProductSection>

<ProductSection product={frontmatter.products[1]} index={1}>
  The Duplex is the shelter the ultralight cottage industry is measured against, and the reason is
  one number: a manufacturer-listed 18.5 oz. That's not a stripped-down claim — it's the full
  canopy with both doors and both vestibules. Dyneema Composite Fabric also has a property spec
  sheets undersell: zero water absorption. A silnylon shelter can pick up several ounces of rain
  water; the Duplex weighs the same on day one and day thirty.

  The trade-offs are real. Single-wall construction means interior condensation is yours to manage
  with site selection and a camp towel. The price band is firmly premium. And thru-hiker owner
  reports are consistent that DCF floors want careful site prep — punctures are easy to patch with
  DCF tape, but you will eventually patch some. Owners who prioritize weight above all keep buying
  it anyway, and its sheer prevalence on long trails is its own durability data set.
</ProductSection>

<ProductSection product={frontmatter.products[2]} index={2}>
  The Lanshan 2 copies the classic two-pole, two-door layout and executes it in silnylon at a
  price band the other two can't approach. Verified owner reviews are consistent on the essentials:
  it keeps rain out, the bug netting is sound, and the layout works. For a first backpacking
  shelter, or for testing whether trekking-pole pitching suits you at all, the downside risk is a
  fraction of the alternatives.

  The compromises are the known silnylon ones plus budget-manufacturing ones. The fabric sags when
  wet, so a taut evening pitch needs re-tensioning in sustained rain. The stock stakes and guylines

  Durston or Zpacks — the standard advice from owners is to pitch it at home and seam-check it the
  week it arrives, not the night before a trip.

## How to choose a trekking pole tent

Start with the weather you actually camp in, not the weather you imagine. If your trips include
sustained rain — the AT, Scotland, shoulder-season anywhere — the X-Mid 2's dry-pitch double-wall
design removes the two failure modes beginners hit hardest: a soaked inner during setup and
condensation on a sleeping bag. Read our guide to
[sleeping bag temperature ratings](/guides/sleeping-bag-temperature-ratings-explained/) for why a
damp bag is a bigger deal than a damp tent.

If you're counting grams for a long trail, the Duplex's math is hard to argue with: the ounces it
saves are ounces you carry every single mile, and its price amortizes differently over a
2,000-mile hike than over three weekends a year. Just budget for the learning curve — and note
that pack fit changes when your shelter drops two pounds; our
[backpack fitting guide](/guides/how-to-fit-a-backpack/) covers re-checking torso and hipbelt
settings after a big base-weight cut.

If the budget is the constraint, buy the Lanshan 2, upgrade its stakes, and spend the savings on
the gear where cheap actually hurts — like your [water filter](/gear/water/best-backpacking-water-filters/)
and sleep insulation. A shelter this serviceable at this price band is the strongest argument
against waiting a season to start backpacking.

## The ounces the spec sheet hides

Listed trail weight is the start of the accounting, not the end. All three shelters need real
decisions that move the number on your scale:

**Stakes.** Trail weight typically excludes them, and a trekking-pole shelter is *entirely*
stake-dependent — there is no freestanding fallback. The X-Mid's rectangle pitches on four
stakes but wants six-to-eight for storm guylines; the Duplex and Lanshan want six minimum and
eight in weather. Budget roughly half an ounce per quality stake, and note the Lanshan's stock
stakes are the first thing owners replace. Skimping here is how shelters end up in the next
county; a shelter you must chase is not ultralight.

**Seam sealing.** The Lanshan ships unsealed — silicone seam sealing is a mandatory
kitchen-table project before its first wet night, and it adds a small amount of weight along
with an evening of your time. The Duplex's DCF seams are taped from the factory; the X-Mid
arrives seam-sealed. Owner reviews that call the Lanshan leaky almost always turn out to
describe an unsealed one, which is a documentation failure more than a fabric one.

**Pole heights.** All three pitch on adjustable trekking poles set to specific heights
(recorded in each maker's setup guide — and worth writing on a piece of tape on your pole).
Fixed-length poles complicate all three shelters; check your pole length against the
manufacturer's stated pitch height before buying, not after.

**The footprint question.** Skip it by default. The weight of a groundsheet cancels a real
fraction of what you paid to save, and the ultralight consensus reflected across owner reports
is that careful site selection protects floors better than a second floor does. The exception
is the Duplex if you camp on granite slab or desert grit — DCF's puncture pattern makes a
piece of window film (lighter than any sold footprint) a defensible ounce.

Where does that leave the actual carried weights? Honestly: add roughly 3–5 oz of stakes and
guyline to every listed figure above, plus sealant on the Lanshan. The ranking between the
three doesn't change — the Duplex's margin is far bigger than any stake kit — but if you're
building a spreadsheet, build it with the real numbers.

One honest caveat that applies to all three: every weight above is the manufacturer's published
figure, recorded in our research file with its source. Seam-sealing (required on the Lanshan,
optional elsewhere), stake swaps, and guyline changes all move real-world weight by an ounce or
two. Weigh your own shelter before you build a spreadsheet around it.

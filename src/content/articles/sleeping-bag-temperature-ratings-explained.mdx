Sleeping bag temperature ratings are the most misread numbers in backpacking, and the
misreading always goes the same direction: cold. A bag marketed with a big "20°F" on the
label is usually citing its *limit* rating — a number that describes a warm-sleeping man
curled in a ball, not shivering, technically asleep. If you shopped believing that number
meant "comfortable at 20°F," you didn't buy the wrong bag; you read a different spec than
the one printed.

This guide explains what the standardized numbers measure, where they break down, and how to
choose a rating for real trips. No products are recommended here — this is the knowledge
that makes spec sheets legible.

## The three numbers the standard produces

Modern bags from larger manufacturers are tested under **ISO 23537** (successor to the older
EN 13537 — the protocols are close enough that ratings are comparable across both). A heated,
sensor-covered mannequin sleeps in the bag, on an insulated pad, in a cold chamber, wearing a
base layer. The test yields three temperatures:

- **Comfort** — where a standard "cold sleeper" (modeled on an average adult woman) sleeps
  relaxed, on her back, without cold. This is the honest planning number for most people.
- **Lower limit** — where a standard "warm sleeper" (modeled on an average adult man) can
  sleep curled up without shivering. Marketing loves this number because it's 8–12°F more
  impressive than comfort.
- **Extreme** — a survival figure: six hours for the standard woman without hypothermia
  death, with shivering and possible cold injury expected. It is not a use rating under any
  circumstance. If a bag's extreme rating is doing work in your planning, change the plan.

The practical translation: **when a label shouts one number, find out which of the three it
is before comparing bags.** Two honest manufacturers can print "20°F" on bags that differ by
a full season of warmth.

## What the test cannot tell you

The ISO test is genuinely useful — it's the only apples-to-apples warmth comparison the
industry has — but it standardizes away the variables that decide your actual night:

**You are not the mannequin.** Metabolism, body composition, exhaustion, hydration, and how
much you ate all move your personal rating by many degrees. Track your own data: after each
cold night, note the low temperature and what you slept in. Three trips of notes beat any
label.

**The test pad is warm.** The standard test uses an insulated pad. Pair a comfort-rated 20°F
bag with a summer pad on frozen ground and the ground takes the argument. Pad R-value and bag
rating form one system — budget ounces across both, the way you'd budget
[stove and fuel weight together](/gear/cooking/best-canister-stoves-backpacking/) rather
than optimizing the stove alone.

**Moisture is unmodeled.** Down that has absorbed a week of body vapor and tent condensation
lofts lower and sleeps colder. This is a shelter problem as much as a bag problem — wall
construction and ventilation differences are a big part of our
[trekking-pole tent comparison](/gear/shelters/best-ultralight-trekking-pole-tents/) — and
it's the strongest practical argument for drying gear at lunch stops.

**Small-maker ratings aren't standardized.** Cottage quilt and bag makers often skip ISO
testing — it's expensive and their designs don't fit the protocol. Their ratings can still
be honest, but they're estimates. For unstandardized products, compare **fill power × fill
weight** (how much down, how lofty) instead of printed temperatures.

## Reading a spec page in sixty seconds

Once you know the three numbers exist, a bag listing decodes fast. The checklist, in order:

1. **Find which rating the headline number is.** Search the spec table for the words
   "comfort," "limit," or "ISO 23537 / EN 13537." If the page names a temperature but no
   standard and no comfort/limit split, treat the number as marketing until proven otherwise.
2. **Check the tested size.** Ratings and weights are usually quoted for the regular size;
   long and wide versions weigh more and occasionally test slightly differently.
3. **Find fill power AND fill weight together.** Fill power (loft per ounce of down) means
   nothing without fill weight (how many ounces are in there). A high-fill-power bag with
   minimal fill weight is a light summer bag wearing a premium label. Two bags with the same
   comfort rating and wildly different fill weights are telling you one of them is optimistic.
4. **Note the shell and construction line.** Sewn-through baffles are lighter and colder
   (seam lines are insulation gaps); box baffles cost weight and hold warmth. This one line
   of the spec table explains many rating differences between similar-looking bags.
5. **Ignore "season" labels entirely.** "Three-season" has no test behind it, varies by
   brand, and mostly encodes what the marketing team thought the buyer wanted to hear. The
   comfort temperature is the same claim with a standard attached — read that instead.

Sixty seconds, five checks, and you've extracted everything the listing honestly knows. If a
listing fails checks one and three — no named standard, no fill weight — that absence is
itself information about the manufacturer.

## Choosing a rating for real trips

The working rule: **buy for the coldest night you'll plausibly see, using the comfort
rating, then add margin for how you sleep.**

1. Find the historical overnight lows for your destination and season — not the daytime
   forecast. Mountain valleys routinely run 10–20°F below nearby town forecasts on clear
   nights.
2. Shop by comfort rating at or below that number. Cold sleepers: subtract another 5–10°F.
3. Remember clothing is part of the system. The test assumes a base layer; sleeping in a
   puffy legitimately extends a bag several degrees. It's the cheapest warmth you own,
   because it's weight you were carrying anyway — the same double-duty logic that makes a
   [well-fitted pack](/guides/how-to-fit-a-backpack/) feel lighter without removing an ounce.

The last honest note: warmth ratings fail in the direction that ruins trips. A bag that's
too warm costs you an unzipped vent and a few carried ounces. A bag that's too cold costs
you sleep, judgment, and sometimes safety. When two ratings are candidates, the colder
number is the adult choice.

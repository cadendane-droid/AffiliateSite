Learning how to fit a backpack correctly is free, takes twenty minutes, and improves every mile
you ever hike — which makes it strange that most people skip it and buy a lighter pack instead.
A properly fitted mid-weight pack carries better than a badly fitted ultralight one, every
time. This guide is the fit process itself: measuring, setting, and adjusting. No products, no
links to buy anything in the body of this page — just the procedure.

## Step 1: Measure your torso length (not your height)

Pack sizes are keyed to torso length, and torso length has only a loose relationship to how
tall you are. You'll need a friend and a flexible tape measure.

1. Tilt your head forward and find the bony bump at the base of your neck. That's the C7
   vertebra — the top of the measurement.
2. Put your hands on your hip bones, thumbs pointing at your spine. The imaginary line between
   your thumbs crosses your spine at the bottom of the measurement (the iliac crest).
3. Have your friend measure along the curve of your spine between those two points, standing
   upright.

That number in inches is what pack size charts actually want. Manufacturers cut sizes
differently — one maker's Medium is another's Large — so always check the chart, never assume
your usual size.

## Step 2: Set the hipbelt on bone

Every fit problem I can describe in this guide is downstream of the hipbelt. With a framed
pack, the belt's padding should be **centered on the iliac crest** — the shelf of bone your
thumbs found in step one — not around your waist like trousers, and not below the bone like a
tool belt. The top edge of the belt typically rides about an inch above the crest.

Load the pack with realistic weight before judging anything. An empty pack fits like an empty
promise; put 15–25 lb in it. Then: belt on bone, buckle it firmly — snug enough that the pack
moves with your pelvis, loose enough to breathe against.

## Step 3: Shoulder straps — contact, not load

Tighten the shoulder straps until they make even contact over the top and front of your
shoulders, then stop. Their job is holding the pack against your back, not carrying it. Two
checks:

- **Anchor point:** the straps should wrap over your shoulders and meet the pack body 1–2
  inches below the top of your shoulders. If the anchor sits above your shoulders, the torso
  length is too long; if it's several inches below, too short.
- **Gap test:** you should be able to slide a couple of fingers under the strap at your
  collarbone. If the strap is levering down hard there, it's stealing load from your hips.

## Step 4: Load lifters and sternum strap — trim tabs, not winches

Load lifters are the small straps running from the top of the shoulder straps to the pack
frame. Their ideal angle is roughly 45 degrees. Snug them until the pack's top leans subtly
toward you — you're aiming for the load riding close to your spine, not for daylight under the
shoulder straps. Cranking them is the most common over-adjustment in the backcountry; it lifts
the straps off your shoulders and dumps pressure oddly onto the hipbelt.

The sternum strap stops the shoulder straps from sliding outward. Clip it, position it about
an inch below your collarbones, and tension it lightly. If you unclip it and the shoulder
straps splay wide, they're set too wide at the anchor or the pack is too big for you.

## Step 5: Walk, then re-adjust in this order

Fit settles under movement. Walk ten minutes — stairs or a hill if you can — then re-adjust
in the same order every time: **hipbelt, shoulder straps, load lifters, sternum**. Order
matters because each adjustment changes the ones after it. Adjusting load lifters before the
hipbelt is rearranging furniture in a house with no foundation.

## The pain map: what hurts tells you what to fix

Fit problems announce themselves as specific pains, and each points at a specific
adjustment. Read the symptom before touching straps at random:

- **Sore shoulder tops** — the classic. Load is riding on your shoulders instead of your
  hips. Re-seat the hipbelt on bone and re-tighten it first; only then re-set the shoulder
  straps. If it recurs on every hike, suspect a too-long torso setting.
- **Bruised or chafed hip points** — belt too tight, belt padding too thin for the load, or
  the belt sitting on the wrong spot (soft tissue instead of the iliac shelf). Loosen, re-seat
  higher onto bone, and check whether the padding bottoms out when cinched.
- **Numb hands or tingling arms** — shoulder straps are compressing nerves near the
  collarbone. Loosen the straps slightly, snug the load lifters a touch, and check the sternum
  strap isn't dragging the straps inward against your neck arteries.
- **Lower-back ache** — load riding too far behind you. Pack heavy items closer to your
  spine and higher between the shoulder blades, then re-snug the load lifters. A sagging,
  bottom-heavy pack defeats any suspension.
- **Neck strain when looking up the trail** — load lifters over-cranked, pulling the top of
  the pack into the back of your head, or a frame too tall for your torso.
- **Pack swaying with each step** — hipbelt too loose, or the load is packed loose and
  shifting. Compression straps exist for exactly this.

Ten minutes of symptom-led adjustment at the first rest stop fixes most of these. The ones it
doesn't fix belong to the next section.

## When fit problems are actually pack problems

Some signals mean no adjustment will save you: hipbelt padding that bottoms out when snug,
strap anchors that can't reach your shoulder line at any torso setting, a frame that ends
below your iliac crest. Those are sizing or design mismatches — change the pack, not the
straps.
